# Evidence Base — J277 Revision Tool — CUSTOMER FACING

**Why this tool works the way it does.**

Most revision advice is folklore. This tool was built around what research
actually shows about how people remember things — and, just as importantly, it's
honest about the parts that are based on teaching experience rather than evidence.

Everything below is sorted into three groups:

- **Strongly evidenced** — the research came first and shaped the design.
- **Well supported** — chosen for practical reasons, and backed up by research.
- **Teacher's judgement** — based on classroom experience. No strong evidence
  either way, and in one case the evidence is genuinely disputed.

That last group is included deliberately. A tool that claims *everything* about it
is scientifically proven should make you suspicious.

## Strongly evidenced

### Why you have to get each question right three times

When you test yourself, a question doesn't disappear after one correct answer. It
comes back until you've answered it correctly **three times**. Get it wrong, and
the count starts again.

This isn't arbitrary. Researchers tested exactly this question — how many times
is enough? — and found a clear jump. Learners who practised recalling something
until they got it right **twice** remembered about 48% of it a week later. Those
who kept going to **three** correct recalls remembered about 68%. Pushing on to
four only reached 75%, which isn't worth the extra time.

So three is the sweet spot: a big gain over two, and most of what four would give
you.

> Rawson, K. A., & Dunlosky, J. (2011). Optimizing schedules of retrieval practice
> for durable and efficient learning: How much is enough? *Journal of Experimental
> Psychology: General*, 140(3), 283–302.

### Why it stops you and tells you to come back another day

If you're still struggling after several rounds, the tool stops and suggests you
return in a day or two, rather than making you grind on.

This can feel like giving up. It isn't. The same researchers found that
**relearning something later is dramatically faster than learning it the first
time** — under two minutes per concept in a second session, and under a minute by
the fifth. Hammering away in one sitting is the expensive way to remember
something. Coming back tomorrow is the cheap way.

The gap isn't wasted time. The gap is doing the work.

### Why a bad start sends you back to your card

If you get less than half right on your first pass, the tool doesn't just keep
firing questions at you — it sends you back to re-read your card first.

Testing yourself on something you never learnt in the first place doesn't teach
you it; it just rehearses getting it wrong. The research protocol these ideas come
from always pairs testing **with re-studying**. So the tool does too.

### Why it remembers when you last revised each topic

Every topic records when you wrote its cards and when you last tested yourself.

This exists so revision can be spaced sensibly. In one of the largest studies of
its kind, over 1,350 people learned a set of facts, revisited them after gaps
ranging up to three and a half months, and were tested up to a year later.
Performance improved as the gap grew — then declined if the gap got too long.
There's an optimal spacing, and it depends on how long you need to remember.

For a GCSE months away, that means **revisiting topics after days or weeks**, not
cramming them the night before. The tool tracks the dates so it can eventually
tell you what you haven't seen in a while.

> Cepeda, N. J., Vul, E., Rohrer, D., Wixted, J. T., & Pashler, H. (2008). Spacing
> effects in learning: A temporal ridgeline of optimal retention. *Psychological
> Science*, 19(11), 1095–1102.

## Well supported

### Why the whole thing is built on testing yourself, not re-reading

A major review examined ten common study techniques and rated only **two** as
genuinely high-utility: **testing yourself** and **spreading study over time**.

Rated *low* utility: summarising, highlighting, and **re-reading your notes** —
three of the most popular revision methods there are.

This tool is built entirely from the two that work, and deliberately offers no
highlighting or re-reading features. If it feels harder than reading through your
notes, that's the point: the feeling of ease when re-reading is exactly what makes
it ineffective.

> Dunlosky, J., Rawson, K. A., Marsh, E. J., Nathan, M. J., & Willingham, D. T.
> (2013). Improving students' learning with effective learning techniques.
> *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*, 14(1), 4–58.

### Why the quizzes don't count for anything

Nothing you do in the self-test is graded, recorded against you, or shown to
anyone. You can get things wrong as often as you like.

This is the best-evidenced part of the tool for people your age — and it's worth
knowing that these studies were done on **11–18 year olds in real classrooms**, on
their actual subject content, measured against their actual exams. Not university
students in a laboratory.

- In one study with Year 9-aged science students, low-stakes quizzes with feedback
  produced **gains of 13% to 25% on end-of-unit exams** — and the benefit was still
  showing up on end-of-year exams.
- In another, with younger students, quizzed material outperformed material that
  students had simply been shown twice — on their real, graded chapter and
  semester exams.
- A third found that **both multiple-choice and short-answer questions worked**,
  which is why this tool uses both rather than just multiple choice.

**And on the obvious worry — doesn't more testing mean more stress?** Researchers
surveyed **1,408 secondary-age students** who'd spent a year doing regular
low-stakes quizzing. **92% said it helped them learn, and 72% said it made them
*less* nervous about tests and exams.**

Worth being precise: that's what students *reported* about how they felt, rather
than a clinical measurement of anxiety. But it's a large sample saying the same
thing.

> McDaniel, M. A., Agarwal, P. K., Huelser, B. J., McDermott, K. B., & Roediger,
> H. L. (2011). *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 103(2), 399–414.
>
> Agarwal, P. K., D'Antonio, L., Roediger, H. L., McDermott, K. B., & McDaniel,
> M. A. (2014). *Journal of Applied Research in Memory and Cognition*, 3(3), 131–139.

### Why you get told the answer straight away

Every question tells you immediately whether you were right, and shows the correct
answer if you weren't.

Two reasons. First, without feedback, getting something wrong repeatedly can
actually cement the wrong answer in your memory. Second, feedback fixes a problem
almost everyone has: we're bad at judging what we know. Re-reading notes makes you
*feel* prepared. Being asked a question and failing to answer it tells you the
truth. That gap — between feeling ready and being ready — is what catches people
out in exams.

### Why it nags you to come back

Spreading revision over time beats doing it all at once. This is one of the two
high-utility techniques above, and it's why the tool records dates and encourages
return visits rather than one long session.

Testing yourself decides *how* you revise. Spacing decides *when*.

## Teacher's judgement

These are choices based on classroom experience rather than research. Being clear
about which is which is the point.

### Writing the cards out by hand

You might have heard that writing by hand beats typing for memory. **The evidence
for that specific claim is much weaker than people think.** A well-known study
suggested it, but when other researchers ran the experiment again properly, they
found no consistent difference between writing longhand, typing, and — in one
case — taking no notes at all. Their own conclusion was that it's too early to say
one method is better.

So handwriting isn't claimed here as a memory trick. Physical cards are used
because they're genuinely useful: you can shuffle them, sort them into "got it"
and "not yet" piles, carry them around, and revise away from a screen. The benefit
is in **making and testing** the cards — which *is* well evidenced — not in the ink.

### A maximum of five questions per card

Purely practical. More than five and a card becomes cramped and hard to read.

### The five-minute memorise timer

A round number that fits a lesson, and you can skip it whenever you're ready. Not
based on research into ideal study length.

### Points, ranks, badges and unlockable games

Included because they motivate students, and because the previous version of this
tool had them. The research on gamification in education is **mixed** — rewards can
boost engagement, but there's a long-standing concern that they can undermine
genuine interest in a subject. This is a judgement call, not an evidence-based
feature.

### Not accepting abbreviations in typed answers

Typing "MAR" instead of "Memory Address Register" is marked wrong on purpose. OCR
mark schemes generally expect the full term, so accepting the short version would
train a habit that costs marks in the exam. That's exam experience, not learning
science.

## An honest word about how much this will help

Most of the research above is American, and much of it comes from carefully
controlled studies. It's worth knowing what UK evidence reviewers say about that.

The Education Endowment Foundation — England's main education evidence charity —
reviewed this whole field and concluded that these principles **can** improve
learning and that teachers should understand them, **but** that positive results
often come from small or tightly controlled studies, and that we don't yet know
how well they work in ordinary classroom conditions. When tested in realistic
classrooms, the effects tend to be **smaller and more variable** than the headline
figures suggest.

There's a sharper point too, and it applies directly to this tool. Most school
studies of self-testing use questions written by the researchers — often nearly
identical to the questions on the final test. The questions in this tool are
written by your teacher, and they are *not* the questions on your OCR paper. So
the promise here is that practising these questions helps you answer real exam
questions — and that step is a reasonable expectation rather than a proven result.

**What that means in plain terms:** the methods this tool is built on are sound and
much better than re-reading your notes. But nobody should promise you a grade. Use
it, use it regularly, and use it alongside past papers.

> Perry, T., Lea, R., Jørgensen, C. R., Cordingley, P., Shapiro, K., & Youdell, D.
> (2021). *Cognitive science approaches in the classroom: A review of the evidence.*
> Education Endowment Foundation.

## The short version

If you remember nothing else from this document:

1. **Testing yourself beats re-reading.** Always. Even when it feels worse.
2. **Coming back tomorrow beats carrying on tonight.** Gaps help.
3. **Getting it right once isn't enough.** Three times is the target.
4. **Feeling prepared and being prepared are different things.** Only testing
   yourself tells you which one you are.
