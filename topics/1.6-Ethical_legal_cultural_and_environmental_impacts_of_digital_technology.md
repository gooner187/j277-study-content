### 5.1 – Computer Systems in the Modern World

#### Communication

Computer technology transforms communication:

- Keep in touch with friends and family worldwide via email, texting, phone calls, and social networking
- Latest news spreads instantly across the globe
- Real-time information: train times, cinema listings, friend locations — all via smartphones

#### Employment

Computer technology has had a huge impact on employment — many jobs have disappeared and new ones have been created.

Jobs lost to computers:

- Clerical work
- Manufacturing
- Journalism
- Robots may soon surpass humans in routine legal work, language translation, and medical diagnosis
- Physical, interpersonal roles (plumbers, gardeners, physiotherapists) are harder to replace

New jobs created:

- Software and hardware development
- Creation of new products: robots, smart homes, mobile technologies
- Online learning materials and aids for disabled people

#### Shopping

- Online shopping gives customers access to products that were previously difficult to find, as well as everyday items
- This easy access has contributed to the closing of local and national stores, leading to empty high streets in many towns

#### Manufacturing

Computer technology produces items faster, more accurately, and more cheaply than can be done by hand. Examples of manufacturing where technology plays a key role:

- Clothing
- Cars
- Building materials
- Electronics

### Recent Developments in Software

Deep Blue was the first chess-playing computer to beat a world champion — defeating Gary Kasparov in 1996.

Go — a Chinese board game played on a 19x19 grid (significantly more complex than chess) — has long been a challenge for AI. Developing software to beat a human Go champion has been a major AI research objective.

### Driverless Cars

Self-driving cars are being tested on public roads. Key legal and ethical questions include:

- Can the car distinguish between a fox, a child, and a plastic bag, and make appropriate decisions?
- Liability question: if someone is hurt or killed by a driverless car, who is responsible — the manufacturer, the owner, or the person in charge of the car at the time?

### The Rise of Artificial Intelligence

A 2016 YouGov survey for the British Science Association (2,000+ respondents) found:

- 60% think AI and robots will lead to fewer jobs within ten years
- 36% believe AI development poses a threat to the long-term survival of humanity
- People generally do not trust robots in life-critical roles:
  - 53% would not trust robots for surgical procedures
  - 49% would not trust robots to drive public buses
  - 62% would not trust robots to fly commercial aircraft
- 13% of men (vs 6% of women) thought they could be friends with a robot
- 28% of 18–24 year-olds thought robots could be future co-workers; 10% imagined robots as family members

### Environmental Impact of Computers

Environmental considerations are complex — negative impacts of manufacturing are often outweighed by the positive effects of computerised systems managing processes more efficiently.

Negative impacts:

- Carbon footprint from manufacturing computer systems
- Waste products from production
- E-waste: outdated technology is discarded rapidly, potentially containing dangerous chemical elements that leach into ground or water supplies

Questions to consider:

- Does working from home reduce driving and therefore pollution?
- Is working from home more energy-efficient than heating and lighting a large office?
- Do computer-managed engines work more efficiently and produce less pollution?
- Has rapid technology change created a "throw-away society" with huge waste dumps of unwanted products?

#### Computers and Waste

The pace of technology means devices desirable a few years ago are now quickly discarded:

- Discarded devices may contain dangerous chemical elements
- Computer-aided manufacturing creates cheap, disposable products — e.g. paper-laminated plastic coffee cups (largely unrecyclable)
- Up to 3 billion coffee cups are handed out in UK coffee shops each year; fewer than 1% are recycled

### Computers and Safety

Many dangerous jobs can now be done remotely by computers and sensors:

- Bomb disposal robots destroy bombs remotely
- Devices sense poisonous gases in mines
- Weather data collected from mountaintops and inside volcanoes without endangering scientists

Computer-collected data on weather, volcanic activity, and seismic movement enables early warnings of hurricanes and earthquakes, allowing people to be moved to safety.

### 5.2 – Ethical, Cultural and Environmental Issues

Computers are now so widespread that we cannot imagine life without them. Key cultural questions include:

- Have we become more or less caring as a society?
- Do we work less hard now computers do so many jobs, or harder than ever?
- Has it become more acceptable to be rude or bully strangers online?
- Does the increase in doorbell video cameras increase security or reduce privacy?

### Computers in Healthcare

Computers play a hugely important role in healthcare:

- Administrative tasks
- Monitoring patients, administering drugs, diagnosing illnesses
- Over 320,000 health-related apps run on iOS and Android
- These apps have been downloaded millions of times by people checking symptoms

Ethical concerns:

- Some health apps may share patients' data without their knowledge
- Computer-aided diagnosis tools need ethical scrutiny as well as scientific/economic evaluation
- Key question: would we accept a diagnostic tool that saved lives overall but discriminated against certain patients?
- Who is responsible for decisions made by computer-based diagnostic tools?

### Social Networking

Benefits:

- Stay in touch with friends and family worldwide
- Share photographs and comments instantly
- Meet new people through internet dating and social networks
- Access information, book tickets, find locations, arrange travel

Concerns:

- People may be losing the ability to socially interact face-to-face
- A Parent Zone survey found:
  - 74.5% of teenagers report the Internet made them happy
  - 36% admitted they would suggest friends avoid Facebook when feeling worried or upset
- Digital exposure carries mental health risks

### The Rating Culture

Digital platforms allow users to rate drivers, restaurants, films, hotels, and people.

Case study — Uber driver ratings:

- Passengers rate drivers; drivers rate passengers
- Passengers act as unpaid supervisors, monitoring driver behaviour
- A driver's score falling below 4.6 out of 5 can result in deactivation
- Ratings can be influenced by factors beyond the driver's control (e.g. type of car, knowledge of back streets)

Key questions raised:

- Is it fair to rate people using criteria they cannot fully control?
- Should a 4.6 out of 5 — a very good score — be grounds for losing employment?
- What responsibility do platforms have for fair rating systems?

### Disinformation and Fake News

Fake content online:

- Viral food hack videos may be dangerous — e.g. a video showing how to make a strawberry white using bleach (ineffective and dangerous)
- False health, science, and news content spreads rapidly

Political disinformation:

- A 2019 CIGI-IPSOS global survey found 89% of people in Canada and Great Britain cited social media as a major reason for not trusting the Internet
- 86% said they had fallen for fake news at least once; 44% said it happened frequently
- Social media increasingly used to influence elections
- Twitter (2018): thousands of fake accounts broadcast millions of tweets to influence voters
- A Commission on Fake News found only 2% of children have the critical skills to identify real vs fake news
- Two-thirds of teachers believe fake news is harming children's well-being and increasing anxiety

- Consequences include depression, fear, and suicide

### The Data Protection Act 2018

The Act contains key principles that all organisations holding personal data must follow. It is designed to protect people's personal data.

The seven principles:

1. Fair, lawful, and transparent processing — personal data must be processed lawfully and fairly for the purpose it was acquired, and with the individual's consent
2. Purpose limitation — data must only be collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes
3. Data minimisation — data must be adequate, relevant, and not excessive; must not be retained longer than necessary
4. Accuracy — data must be accurate and up-to-date
5. Data retention periods — data must not be retained longer than necessary; if a person asks for it to be erased, it must be securely deleted or destroyed
6. Data security — data must be kept secure, including protection against unauthorised or unlawful processing and against accidental loss, destruction, or damage
7. Accountability — data controllers must be able to prove that their data protection measures are sufficient; appropriate technical and organisational measures must be in place

### The Computer Misuse Act 1990

Primarily designed to prevent unauthorised access or hacking of programs or data. The Act recognised the following offences:

1. **Unauthorised access to computer material**
   - What it prohibits: logging into someone else's account, guessing a password to access a system, or using someone's computer without permission — even if no damage is caused and nothing is stolen, the access itself is a criminal offence

2. **Unauthorised access with intent to commit or facilitate a crime**
   - What it prohibits: accessing a system without permission with the intention of committing a further crime (e.g. accessing a bank's system to commit fraud, or accessing a computer to steal data)
   - This is more serious than Offence 1 — the intent to commit the further crime is enough, even if the further crime is never actually carried out

3. **Unauthorised modification of computer material**
   - What it prohibits: deleting, changing, or encrypting someone else's files without permission — this includes installing malware, deleting data, or defacing a website
   - Releasing a virus that damages files falls under this offence

4. **Making, supplying, or obtaining anything which can be used in computer misuse offences**
   - What it prohibits: creating, sharing, or downloading hacking tools, malware, or exploit code — even if they are never used
   - Possessing tools designed for hacking is itself a criminal offence under this provision

### The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988

Designed to protect the creators of books, music, videos, and software from having their work illegally copied.

- Makes it illegal to use, copy, or distribute commercially available software without buying the appropriate licence
- When a computer system is designed and implemented, licensing must be considered

What is illegal if you buy a music CD, download music, software, or a video:

- Passing a copy to a friend
- Making a copy and selling it
- Using the software on a network, unless the licence explicitly allows it

How the software industry prevents illegal copying:

- Requiring the user to enter a unique key before installation
- Some software only runs if the original CD is present in the drive
- Some applications only run if a hardware dongle is plugged into a USB port

### Software Licences

A software licence is a licence agreement giving permission to an individual or organisation to use a piece of software.

- It is usually illegal to buy one copy of a program and copy it to multiple computers
- An organisation may buy a site licence which allows the software to be copied to a specified number of computers

---

### Open Source Software

Open Source software is governed by the Open Source Initiative:

- Licensed for use but there is no charge for the licence — anyone can use it
- Must be distributed with the source code so anyone can modify it
- Developers can sell the software they have created
- Any new software created from Open Source software must also be open — it must be distributable in a form that others can read and edit

---

### Freeware

Freeware is free to use but:

- The user does not get access to the source code
- Usually has restrictions on its use

This is different from Open Source — freeware is free but closed; Open Source may or may not be free but is always open.

---

### Proprietary Software

Proprietary software is sold in the form of a licence to use it.

- The licence may specify only one concurrent user, or permit up to a certain number of users on one site (site licence)
- The company or person who wrote the software holds the copyright
- Users will not have access to the source code
- Users will not be allowed to modify the package and sell it to others — this would infringe the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act
- Benefit: professional support, regular updates, technical support lines, training courses, and a large user base

---

### Software Type Comparison

| Property | Open Source | Freeware | Proprietary |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | Free (usually) | Free | Paid (licence) |
| Source code access | Yes | No | No |
| Can be modified | Yes | No | No |
| Can be redistributed | Yes (with conditions) | Usually restricted | No |
| Support | Community-based | Varies | Professional (company) |
| Examples | Linux, LibreOffice | Adobe Acrobat Reader | Microsoft Office, Adobe Photoshop |

### Privacy Issues

Digital technology raises significant privacy concerns:

- Google collects and stores data about millions of emails every day
- Small computers embedded in everyday objects (clothes, beermats, smart buildings) will create constant data collection — a vision of a world without privacy
- Governments and security agencies collect huge amounts of data about private citizens, often supplied by Internet and telephone companies

Government surveillance:

- The US Government collects, stores, and monitors metadata about electronic communications to detect terrorist or illegal activity
- Metadata includes: telephone number called, date, time, and duration of call
- In one month in 2013, the unit collected data on more than 97 billion emails and 124 billion phone calls from around the world
- Edward Snowden is a famous whistleblower who informed the world about these surveillance practices

### Using Cookies

Cookies are files (often including unique identifiers) sent by web servers to web browsers, which may then be sent back to the server each time the browser requests a page.

Cookies are used:

- To recognise your computer when you revisit a website
- To track you as you navigate the website and enable e-commerce facilities
- To improve the website's usability
- To analyse the use of the website
- In the administration of the website
- To personalise the website, including targeted advertisements

Some websites are legally required to inform users that they collect data via cookies.

### Hacking — Case Study

In May 2015, a computer security expert flying on a passenger airliner hacked into the aircraft's computer controls through the entertainment system and briefly made it fly sideways. He told the FBI he did this to demonstrate vulnerabilities in the computer system so they could be fixed.

This case illustrates:

- The serious potential consequences of security vulnerabilities in safety-critical systems
- The ethical distinction between authorised (penetration testing) and unauthorised hacking — even when the intent is to improve security, unauthorised access to aircraft controls is a criminal offence under the Computer Misuse Act

### Misconceptions

- misconception: Computers always improve the environment because they replace physical processes
  reality: The picture is complex. Manufacturing computers creates carbon emissions and waste. Discarded devices can contain dangerous chemicals. However, computerised systems often reduce pollution in other ways — more efficient engines, remote working, and better environmental monitoring. Neither purely positive nor purely negative.
  trigger: When students say computers are always environmentally beneficial or always harmful
  correction_prompt: The environmental impact of computers is not straightforward. Manufacturing them has a carbon cost and generates hazardous e-waste. But computers also help manage processes more efficiently — reducing pollution from engines, enabling remote working (fewer car journeys), and monitoring the environment. Exam answers should acknowledge both sides.

- misconception: The ethical issues around AI and computers have clear right or wrong answers
  reality: This topic is deliberately open-ended. The OCR spec asks students to consider and discuss ethical, cultural, and environmental issues — not to give definitive answers. Exam questions in this area reward balanced discussion and recognition of multiple perspectives, not single-sided conclusions.
  trigger: When students expect a definitive correct answer to questions about AI, driverless cars, or digital ethics
  correction_prompt: Ethical and cultural questions about computer technology deliberately do not have single correct answers. The exam rewards students who can present multiple perspectives and acknowledge complexity. For example, on driverless cars: there are arguments about liability, safety improvements, and decision-making — none of which has a universally agreed answer. Present the arguments on both sides.

- misconception: Social networking is purely harmful to young people
  reality: The evidence is mixed. 74.5% of teenagers report the Internet made them happy, while 36% would suggest friends avoid Facebook when worried. Social networking has genuine benefits (connectivity, information access, community) alongside real risks (mental health, loss of face-to-face skills, digital exposure).
  trigger: When students characterise social networking as entirely negative
  correction_prompt: Social networking has both benefits and drawbacks. Most teenagers find it makes them happy, but a significant minority associate it with anxiety. A balanced exam answer should acknowledge connectivity, access to information, and community as positives, alongside risks like reduced face-to-face interaction and mental health concerns.

- misconception: Open Source and Freeware are the same thing
  reality: Both may be free to use, but they are fundamentally different. Open Source software provides access to the source code and allows modification and redistribution (with conditions). Freeware is free to use but the source code is not provided and use is usually restricted. A piece of software can be open source but not free, or free but not open source.
  trigger: When students use open source and freeware interchangeably
  correction_prompt: Open Source and Freeware are not the same. Open Source means the source code is provided and can be modified and redistributed — it may or may not cost money. Freeware is free to use but you do not get the source code and cannot modify it. The key distinction is source code access, not price.

- misconception: The Data Protection Act only applies to digital data
  reality: The Data Protection Act 2018 applies to all personal data, including paper records. Any organisation that holds personal data — whether on computers, in filing cabinets, or any other format — must comply with its principles.
  trigger: When students say the DPA only covers data stored on computers
  correction_prompt: The Data Protection Act covers all personal data, not just data stored on computers. An organisation that keeps paper records with personal information is also bound by the Act's principles. The law protects people's personal data regardless of the format it is stored in.

- misconception: Using open source software always means you can do anything you want with it
  reality: Open source software comes with licences that set conditions on use. The key rule: if you create new software from open source code, it must also be open. You cannot take open source code, modify it, and release a closed proprietary version.
  trigger: When students think open source means no restrictions at all
  correction_prompt: Open source software does come with conditions. The most important one is that if you create new software using open source code, that new software must also be open — you cannot take open source code and release it as closed proprietary software. The licence protects the openness of the software and its derivatives.
